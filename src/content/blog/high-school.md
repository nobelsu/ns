I hate goodbyes. Something about that deplorable word carries an implicit ending of sorts. The ending of a weekday, the ending of a hangout, the ending of a trip, and what’s been more recently bothersome for me: the ending of high school. It’s been months since graduation, so why do I still keep mulling over it?

So often do we add a “see you later” at the end of every conversation. Three words that, meaningless alone, together attach continuity to closings. Three words that, pointlessly overlooked, together reassure us that our story isn’t over, that we’re merely flipping to the next page, that we’re yet to arrive at the final chapter.

But what happens when you reach the back cover? What happens when you run out of writing space? When the lines run short and the words run few and far in between? Suddenly, those three words feel heavy. Suddenly, the silence feels empty. It gets quiet; it gets lonely. “See you later” sounds more like “stay with me”, even more so like “don’t leave me”. “I’ll miss you” comes running out of our mouths, then there’s this bittersweet realization that nothing might ever be the same.

They say that if we keep reminiscing about the past, we’ll miss out on the current moments. “So live in every moment, and don’t turn back.” But alas, at this moment, may I refuse?

I’ll miss the library balcony, the umbrella at the fountain, the hut, the collab rooms, Pak Tri’s classroom or the hexagon tables. These were our places of convening after all. The Among Us games we’d play, the dumb conversations, grabbing coffee at Kumpul or Vive, our shared honor passes, rage baiting and getting rage baited, freely touring the campus as if we didn’t have a hundred essays left to write.

I’ll miss coming home to ‘5 missed calls from “the MANDEM”’, knowing fully well someone’s on there gambling their life savings away. The declined voice calls, saying we’re “busy”, but really, we’re ridiculously unemployed. Or the incessant physical harassment (wait, since when did we start this?), the mandatory nightly crash outs, the scuffed Rich Up WhatsApp group calls, church lunches, weekend dinners, the chaotic karaoke sessions, fighting over who gets to “money launder”. 

I’ll miss normalizing “weird”, being unfiltered versions of ourselves without care, because no one was truly watching, and we knew it. 

But all is said and done. From the other side of the pond, I wonder, when will we next meet? Months, maybe years. Even then, nothing will ever be the same. We can’t go back to being high school students, because that moment has gone by us. This book is closed, the next is to be awaited.

Still, one thing is for certain: I may not have found the mother of my children, but damn right have I found their uncs.

So for now, we say to Jakarta, ALDOS OUT ✌️
